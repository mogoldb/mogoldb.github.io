NFL Team Building Strategy - Optimising Receiver Corps

Mo Goldberger

Introduction

This offseason in the National Football League (NFL), two teams, the Chiefs and the Packers, who have two of the best receivers in the league, Tyreek Hill and Devante Adams, have traded those players away. In turn, they got many draft picks, and it seems these teams' strategies are to have more good receivers rather than having 1-2 star receivers, with the rest of the receiving corps being significantly below average. This brings up an interesting team-building strategy question in a salary-capped league: is it better to have a top-heavy receiving corps with superstars that change the math of the game, or is it better to have a unit of more reliable receivers without one gamechanger?

First, I'll explain a little bit about the salary cap in the NFL. According to Bleacher Report, "A salary cap is essentially an agreement between the league and players that places a limit on the amount of money a team can spend on salaries for players. The NFL uses a hard cap, meaning that no team is allowed to exceed the cap limit for any reason." The cap balances the league, ensuring that teams have to employ strategy in team building rather than being able to buy the best team possible, which can create a boring league with the wealthiest teams dominating the game.

The salary cap forces teams to prioritize where they put their money. Building a team is like recruiting. You want the best players to come to you, and one of the ways to do that is by paying them the most money. But you also want to make sure that you are not putting too much money in one area or player because if you do so, the salary cap will prevent you from investing as much in the rest of the team. Two big things that need to be taken into account when thinking about money allocation in team building are positional value and the value of each individual player. Pro Football Focus (PFF), a football analytics company, has a metric called Wins Above Replacement (WAR), which estimates roughly how many wins an individual player has contributed to his team. Meaning, that you should subtract a player's WAR from the team's total wins to say how many wins the team would have if you were to replace the player with an average one. WAR is a way of calculating the value of a specific player but also tells us a lot about positional value. This chart from PFF shows the average WAR of the top 10 players from each position from 2019:

Data shows that in the modern NFL, which is a passing league, after Quarterback, wide receivers are the most valuable position on the team. While there is a lot of data on individual player value and positional value, there isn't a lot of research on what combinations of players make for the best position group. As in, what combination of wide receivers gives you the best receiving corps? On the one hand, receivers can completely change the math of the game, especially when it comes to star receivers. When one receiver needs two defenders on him at all times or is so fast that the opposing team needs to adjust their entire defensive scheme for him, he's valuable and impacting the game even when he doesn't touch the ball.

On the other hand, it's just one player, which great defensive coaches can figure out how to shut down. And if that happens, while the remainder of your receiving corps is not very reliable due to the money spent on the star, is it better to have 2 or 3 above average players than being top-heavy? Were the Cheifs and Packers right in opting to pay one individual player less and spreading their money across receivers more equally, or did the teams that acquired Tyreek Hill and Devante Adams have the right idea?

In this tutorial, we will inspect PFF data and develop a model that can take in the performance grades of 4 receivers and predict the total receiving corp performance based on the performance. This can allow general managers (GMs) to look at the receivers they have and run the model with different free-agent receivers and try to predict the total performance of those hypothetical receiving corps.

Part 1: Gathering the data¶

First, we need to gather and decide what data to use. While many people look at standard statistics in football, like touchdowns, percentage of targets caught, and yards per reception, many of these traditional statistics are flawed and don't provide context. Firstly, it only evaluates a player's performance when targeted, so it doesn't account for how they impact the rest of the game. Additionally, football is the ultimate team sport, and a lot of those traditional statistics are dependent on the performance of everyone around the receiver, not just the receiver's performance. So, for example, if a receiver completely runs the wrong route, the quarterback finds him despite his mistake, and he gets a touchdown; the receiver gets credit even though he did something wrong. PFF evaluates every player on every play, which helps account for their performance when players are not touching the ball. In addition, this grading method isolates player performance, so players don't get penalized for other players' mistakes or credit for other players' great plays. These grades are out of 100, and usually, anything above 90 is considered elite, but the average grade varies with position. PFF also has grades for team units, like grades for a team's offense, defense, receiving corp, secondary, etc. So we will use individual player grades and the team receiving corps grades to train our model.

One consideration we have to make is how many years of data do we want? On the one hand, more data helps us train our model better, but on the other hand, the game of football has rapidly changed over the past 5-10 years, so going too far back can train the model with data that is no longer relevant to today's NFL. To decide how far back to go, I wanted to find a point from one year to another that made it clear the NFL started valuing receivers significantly more. I looked at overthecap.com and how much the teams that spend the most money on receivers spent. There was a big jump from 2016 to 2017:

             2015            |             2016             |            2017

As you can see, the shift from 2016 to 2017 is far more drastic than from 2015 to 2016 and generally consistent with the general shift around football to even more of an emphasis on passing. As a fan, it's clear to see how the game has changed over the past few years and the explosion of passing offenses, especially with so many young talented quarterbacks with really strong arms. This influx started around the 2017 season, and 2017 feels like a good place to define a starting point for this newer era of football.

Getting Reciever Grades¶

First, we will collect the receiver grades from PFF. I decided to include tight ends (TE) in the data because a few tight ends in the league fill the role of receiver. For example, the Cheifs can justify trading away Tyreek Hill because they have Travis Kelce, a TE who is one of the league's best receivers. Calculating the Cheif's receiving corp without him would not make sense because he is now their best receiver. On the other hand, TEs like Kelce are an anomaly, and not all TEs get the number of targets as him. To prevent TEs or WRs who are buried on the depth chart from skewing the data, only the grades of the receivers with the top 4 targets will go into the model, but I will explain more about that later.

But this merge isn't exactly what we wanted. We need each team and year combination to be one row that contains the team passing grade and the top 4 players on each team's individual grades. We need to do a bit more manipulation. We will go through each team and then add a row for each year associated with that team to a new dataframe we are building.

Additionally, we define the top 4 not by grade but by the number of targets they get. Each player will be ordered in terms of how much they are relied on. So the receiver that gets the most targets is considered the number 1 receiver and not the receiver with the highest grade. If we sorted by the receiver with the highest grade, there could be receivers who have been on the field for two games and got five targets and have an excellent grade. But that doesn't tell us much about the receiving corp and the player's effect on the team.

Part 3: Checking Linear Regression Assumptions¶

While we can run a linear regression on any dataset, and we will get a model that can predict things, those predictions don't mean anything unless the data is linear. If the model doesn't fit the data, it is useless in its predictive powers. We must make sure that the four assumptions of a linear regression are met before using a linear regression on the data. The four assumptions go as follows:

  1. A linear relationship exists
  2. Normality of residuals
  3. No multicollinearity among predictors
  4. No autocorrelation of residuals
  5. Constant Variance of residuals (this is known as 'Homoscedasticity')

We will use techniques discussed in this towards data science and this article to evaluate if these assumptions are accurate.

First, let's establish linearity. We can inspect plots of observed vs. predicted values or residuals vs. predicted values to detect linearity. If the data is linear, we would expect that the points are symmetrically distributed around a diagonal line in the observed vs. predicted values plot and a horizontal line for the residuals vs. predicted values plot. The variance should also be nearly constant.

We will start by using the data we formatted above to make a linear regression model:

def linearity_test(model, y):
    '''
    Function for visually inspecting the assumption of linearity in a linear regression model.
    It plots observed vs. predicted values and residuals vs. predicted values.
    
    Args:
    * model - fitted OLS model from statsmodels
    * y - observed values
    '''
    fitted_vals = model.predict()
    resids = model.resid

    fig, ax = plt.subplots(1,2)
    
    sns.regplot(x=fitted_vals, y=y, lowess=True, ax=ax[0], line_kws={'color': 'red'})
    ax[0].set_title('Observed vs. Predicted Values', fontsize=16)
    ax[0].set(xlabel='Predicted', ylabel='Observed')

    sns.regplot(x=fitted_vals, y=resids, lowess=True, ax=ax[1], line_kws={'color': 'red'})
    ax[1].set_title('Residuals vs. Predicted Values', fontsize=16)
    ax[1].set(xlabel='Predicted', ylabel='Residuals')
    
linearity_test(lin_reg, y)

We expected that if the data were linear, the first plot would have a diagonal line, the second to have a horizontal, and the variance to be mostly constant. 3.2. Testing Normality of Residuals¶

There are several ways to evaluate the normality of the residuals. We can look at QQ plots of the residuals. A QQ plot plots two sets of quantiles against each other if they both come from the same distribution, the points should form a roughly straight line. So let's inspect this QQ plot to see if it looks like a straight line.

3.4. Testing no Autocorrelation of Residuals¶

Next, we need to see if there is autocorrelation in the residuals. We can test that by performing the Durbin-Watson to detect if there is a positive or negative correlation on the residuals. When the test produces values between 1.5 and 2.5, there is no autocorrelation. When the results are between 0 and 2, there is positive autocorrelation, and when the result is from 2 to 4, there is a negative autocorrelation.

3.5 Testing for Homoscedasticity of Residuals¶

Now, let's evaluate homoscedasticity. There are several ways to do so. First, we can look at the residuals vs. predicted values from the linearity test and see if the variance seems to be uniform. For the most part, it did. But we will also run a test called the Breusch-Pagan test. In this test, the null hypothesis assumes homoscedasticity, and a p-value below 0.05 will indicate that we need to reject the null hypothesis of homoscedasticity.

def homoscedasticity_test(model):
    '''
    Function for testing the homoscedasticity of residuals in a linear regression model.
    It runs Breusch-Pagan and Goldfeld-Quandt tests.
    
    Args:
    * model - fitted OLS model from statsmodels
    '''
    fitted_vals = model.predict()
    resids = model.resid
    resids_standardized = model.get_influence().resid_studentized_internal

    bp_test = pd.DataFrame(sms.het_breuschpagan(resids, model.model.exog), 
                           columns=['value'],
                           index=['Lagrange multiplier statistic', 'p-value', 'f-value', 'f p-value'])

    print('\n     ---- Breusch-Pagan test ----')
    print(bp_test)

Part 4: Data Analysis¶

Previously, our model was trained on the entire dataset, so the r2 value was based on values the model had already seen. That means we are evaluating the model's accuracy based on data it is trained on, which is problematic because we want to see how it performs on data it's never seen before. To solve this issue, we will split the data up and train our model on most of the data but preserve a small portion of data to test the model on.

We are attempting to make a data model that can take in the top 4 most productive receivers on a team and predict that receiving corp's overall performance (grade). The model will be provided with the receiver grades as the independent variable, and the receiving unit grade will be the dependent variable.

When initially building the model, I sorted players by grade and only included grades as features. It resulted in a model that was very off. There were also issues when changing the order of the grades put into the model because the model would way WR3 and WR4 more because the coefficients were higher. I wanted a way to normalize that a little more, and though including targets would help. It did! I then realized that choosing the top four players and sorting by grade was a bad idea for the reasons I explained above, so I started sorting by targets. Once I sorted the top four players by targets, I was able to remove targets as a feature the model performed very slightly better. I decided not to include targets as a feature of the model, not because the model performed better that way, but because logically, once the model takes into account that the WR1 has the most targets, WR2 has the second most, and so on. Once the model has a way to weigh each receiver by their place on the depth chart, the exact number of targets is unnecessary. If you can get a model working with fewer features, it's better than adding more noise, so I chose to leave the targets out of the training.

Part 5: Using the Model!¶

def print_prediction(grades):
    '''
    Takes in a dictionary of grades to targets, and runs the model 
    on those grades in order of most to least targets and prints out the result.
    '''
    grade_lst = []
    
    n = len(grades)
    for _ in range(n):
        temp = max(grades, key=lambda k: grades[k])
        grade_lst.append(temp)
        del grades[temp]
    
    prediction = model.predict([grade_lst])

    print('''Receiver corp grade prediction for:
            player 1 grade {},
            player 2 grade {},
            player 3 grade {},
            player 4 grade {},
            is: {}'''.format(grade_lst[0], grade_lst[1], grade_lst[2], grade_lst[3], prediction[0]))

Part 6: Conclusion¶

As we've seen in part 5 shows that even within different receiver corp building strategies of top-heavy vs. above average, it depends on how elite and above-average each of the players are. There is no one strategy that is correct across the board, and we should use the data we have to improve our decisions. Data is incredibly powerful, and its predictive ability can make smart teams build their team even better. Data science has been used in football for a while now regarding game-time decisions and player grades, but much more can be done with team building. Team building in a salary-capped league can be boiled down to an optimization problem. There are so many creative solutions NFL teams can come up with to improve their approach to team building and give them a competitive edge.
